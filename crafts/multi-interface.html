    Side question: How can Multi-sensorial experience affect food neophobia?

    Food neophobia is when one shows extreme aversion toward novel or unfamiliar foods. It is often regarded as a common stage in child's development stage, but many cases have been reported that it can prolong into adulthood, causing nutritional problems. It is often associated with effortlessly liked tastes such as sweet and fatty.
    Previous Measures
    · Hands-on activities: cooking, gardening
    · Sensory education
    · Nutrition education
    · Sensory classes: les classes du goût
    · Digital games


    However, critical limitations were that most programs are long-term and costly, necessitates face-to-face interactions and most importantly, mainly designed for children. Concerning that decent amount of neophobic populations is adults, it is time to search for an appropriate measure to tackle adult food neophobia, one that requires less time for adjustment and that can be easily encountered daily and not in special conditions. An emerging theory is exposing the sensory attributes prior to the consumption of food. Myriad of studies point that it should be an effective means to approach food neophobia without having to cause any tears, considering that the hardest part of food neophobia is to try it in the first place, yet no experiment has been conducted to this end.

    → I was especially interested to see the effects of multi-sensorial exposure to the food neophobic participants, which turned out to be 47% of the total sample in the experiment. Out of which over 70% was adults, and therefore, it was worthwhile to observe their interactions, effects and if the theories on such a topic were proven valid. If so, I wanted to continue on researching the effect of multi-sensorial interfaces to sensorially impaired and older groups of participants.

    → This is why I wanted to first understand if an empirical workshop on mapping sensory attributes is viable, and incorporating the multi-sensorial technologies can be perceived as effective toward, but not limited to, food neophobia.
